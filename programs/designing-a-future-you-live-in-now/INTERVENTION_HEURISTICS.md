# Intervention Heuristics

## Purpose
Once a barrier is identified, use a simple intervention with a clear reason.

## Heuristics
### 1. The five-second move
When the participant feels the impulse to act, pair it with immediate motion before avoidance takes over.

**Use when:** they know what to do but stall instantly.

### 2. The twenty-hour ramp
Break a skill into core subskills and commit to focused early practice long enough to cross the frustration barrier.

**Use when:** the participant gives up because they hate being a beginner.

### 3. The three-path future
Require more than one future option so identity and hope do not collapse into a single narrow plan.

**Use when:** the participant feels trapped by one imagined outcome.

### 4. Prototype before commitment
Try the conversation, the class, the job shadow, the task, or the mini-version before demanding certainty.

**Use when:** fear is inflated by abstraction.

### 5. Reduce the step size
Make the next move smaller until action becomes possible.

**Use when:** overwhelm is blocking motion.

### 6. Convert comparison into evidence
Move from "I am behind" into "what data do I actually have?"

**Use when:** identity collapse and timeline panic show up.

## Matching table
- Frozen -> five-second move
- Beginner frustration -> twenty-hour ramp
- Single-future collapse -> three-path future
- Overwhelm -> reduce the step size
- Fantasy or fear loop -> prototype before commitment
- Shame spiral -> convert comparison into evidence

## Mentor note
A good intervention is not impressive. It is effective.
